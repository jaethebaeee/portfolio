Visual Style: Clean, Simple, and Modern

Even with limited resources, we can give our videos a professional and modern look by making smart design choices. Since you mentioned keeping the design “clean and simple,” we should likely use a minimalistic style rather than overloading the viewer with busy graphics. Here are some guidelines for our visual design:

Consistent, minimalist graphics: We can stick to a cohesive set of fonts, colors, and graphic elements across all videos for brand identity. A modern look often means flat design (solid colors, simple shapes) and lots of open space. For instance, if we show text, it could appear on a neutral background with a clear, sans-serif font. If we use icons or illustrations, they should have a uniform style (all hand-drawn, or all flat vector art, etc., but not a mishmash). Consistency will make the video feel polished even if each element is simple.

Focus on key visuals: Rather than trying to animate every single thing being said, we can selectively visualize the most important or most tricky concepts. This keeps the screen uncluttered. For example, in a video about memory techniques, we might mostly have voiceover with subtle background graphics, but when we hit a critical point (like demonstrating how a mnemonic works), we bring up a clear diagram or animation for that. By not over-animating, we also save time and make the visuals more impactful when they do appear.

Use metaphors and images smartly: You mentioned “tie it with some psychological topics” – indeed, using metaphoric imagery can make abstract ideas concrete. If we talk about, say, “the mind’s defense mechanisms,” a simple metaphor might be a shield icon or a short scene of a castle wall appearing, to represent defenses. We can incorporate stock photos or short video clips as backgrounds to symbolize ideas (for instance, a footage of a frustrated person in an office could play faintly behind our text when discussing stress at work). Since we want to keep it clean, we might render these visuals in a semi-transparent or monochromatic way so they don’t distract, but rather complement the narration. The idea is to enrich the storytelling visually without feeling like clutter.

Crucially, “modern” design often also means smooth, high-quality motion. Simple does not equal static – gentle movements or transitions can maintain interest. For example, instead of a jarring cut from one graphic to another, we might use a sleek fade-in or slide-in animation. If we display a quote or a definition, we could animate it with a brief “typewriter” effect (letters appearing as if being typed). In fact, since you suggested making it seem like we are writing when explaining metaphors, we have tools to do that – the animation library Manim (which we already use) has a Write() function that literally draws text on-screen stroke by stroke, giving the effect of handwriting or typing. We can use that to our advantage to keep the “written” metaphors feeling dynamic. For example, if the script says, “Imagine the word ‘memory’ written on a foggy mirror,” we could actually animate the word memory appearing letter-by-letter, as if a finger is writing it on glass. Little touches like that keep viewers visually engaged with minimal graphics.

Because we’re a one-man team, simplicity will also aid our production workflow. A clean style means fewer elements to animate per scene and fewer things that can go wrong. It also means we can create templates – for instance, a basic layout where we always place a title at the top, an illustration on the side, and maybe a progress bar or chapter indicator at the bottom. We can reuse such layouts for each video, just swapping out the text and images. This makes production faster over time while maintaining a consistent modern aesthetic.

Concrete before Abstract: We touched on this earlier, but it’s crucial. Hook viewers with a story or example first, then generalize. Grant explicitly advises educators to present specific cases or visuals before formal definitions or abstract statements
